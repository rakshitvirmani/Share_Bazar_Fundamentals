## 1. Business Model

TD Power Systems (TDPS) is a Bengaluru-headquartered manufacturer of AC generators (alternators) and electric motors. The company was founded in 1999 and has built a niche position as one of the very few non-European, non-Japanese companies supplying generators to global OEMs at scale for the power generation market.

### Core Products

TDPS manufactures generators and alternators that are paired with gas turbines, gas engines, steam turbines, and hydro turbines by global OEMs who then sell the complete turbine-generator set to end customers (data centers, power plants, grid operators, etc.). The company also makes traction motors for the railway segment and industrial motors for various applications.

### Revenue Segments

The business can be broken down into the following segments: generators and motors (the core manufacturing business contributing roughly 80-85% of the order book), railways (traction motors for Indian Railways via Alstom and export markets in Europe, US, and Russia contributing around 15%), spares and aftermarket (a small but recurring segment), and the Turkey subsidiary (which has been winding down its local market operations but is being repurposed as a tariff-mitigation hub for US-bound exports).

### Geographic Mix

The geographic mix has shifted decisively towards exports over the past two years. As of 9M FY26, 79% of order inflows are from exports and deemed exports, with only 21% domestic. Key export markets are the US (driven by data centers, AI server farms, and gas-based captive power), Europe (grid stabilization, renewables backup, and increasingly data centers), and CIS countries. The domestic market is primarily captive power plants (steam turbines for steel, cement, etc.), small hydro (up to 45 MW), and biomass/waste heat recovery.

### Customer Relationships

TDPS works with 3 major global OEMs who manufacture gas turbines and gas engines. These OEMs (the largest being the company's primary customer) package TDPS generators with their turbines/engines and sell complete sets to end users. This B2B2C model means TDPS is deeply embedded in these OEMs' supply chains. The OEMs are booking capacity with TDPS for FY27 and FY28, indicating strong long-term demand visibility. The company is also close to acquiring a new US customer in the gas turbine segment.

### Manufacturing Footprint

TDPS operates from its Bengaluru campus with 3 plants. The third plant was declared operational on December 18, 2025, significantly expanding capacity. The company also has a plant in Turkey (100% subsidiary) which has been operating for 7 years and is being repositioned to handle assembly and testing for US-bound exports to mitigate tariff impacts. Additionally, a UK-based design center has been established to develop larger generators in the 50-150 MW range using high-caliber engineering talent.

### Competitive Positioning

TDPS has a significant cost advantage over European and Japanese competitors. Using management's own illustration: if TDPS's product costs 100, a European equivalent costs approximately 125. With a 15% EU tariff to the US, the European product costs 150 delivered. TDPS at 100 (or even 125-130 when routed via Turkey with a 15% duty) maintains a 15-20% cost arbitrage. There is virtually no US manufacturing capability for generators above 10-12 MW, making imports essential. TDPS is the only large non-European/non-Japanese supplier to the US market, giving it a unique positioning.

## 3. Red Flags

### Red Flag 1: Concentration Risk on Global OEMs - Rating: 7/10

TDPS's gas turbine/gas engine generator business (the fastest growing and most exciting segment) depends heavily on 3 global OEMs, with one having the largest share. If this primary OEM shifts sourcing or develops in-house generator capability, TDPS would face a severe revenue hit. Management has acknowledged they work with all 3 big OEMs but the primary customer dominates order flow. The mitigant is that all OEMs are capacity-constrained and TDPS offers cost and delivery advantages, but the concentration remains a structural risk.

### Red Flag 2: US Tariff Uncertainty - Rating: 6/10

The US tariff situation has been a recurring theme across all 4 quarters. During Q1 FY26, tariffs from India jumped to 50%, forcing TDPS to activate "Plan B" of routing production through Turkey. By Q3 FY26, management indicated customers are paying the tariff and no one is insisting on Turkey re-routing anymore. However, tariff policy remains unpredictable. If tariffs escalate further or a trade deal collapses, the additional logistics cost of Turkey routing (roughly 15% higher cost base) could compress margins on that portion of business. The positive is that tariffs impact only the 4-5% of business that is direct US exports (75% of US-bound product goes through European OEMs who re-export from Europe at 15% duty).

### Red Flag 3: Turkey Operational Risk - Rating: 5/10

Turkey has been described as a difficult geography with high company bankruptcy rates. TDPS has been there for 7 years and knows the landscape, but they are now expanding Turkish operations for tariff-related reasons. The Turkish lira is highly unstable (management explicitly avoids lira-denominated business). The Turkish domestic market is described as "very bleak" and the plant was being considered for shutdown before tariffs made it strategically useful again. If the tariff situation resolves, this plant becomes a stranded cost again.

### Red Flag 4: Weak Free Cash Flow During Growth Phase - Rating: 5/10

Management has been transparent that free cash flow will be weak during this high-growth phase. CFO-to-EBITDA conversion has been below 10%. All retained earnings are going back into inventory and working capital to support 30%+ growth. While this is logical and the company isn't borrowing, it means shareholders see profit growth but limited cash generation. Management expects this to continue as long as high growth sustains.

### Red Flag 5: Motor Business De-Prioritized - Rating: 4/10

The motor business was supposed to be a major growth lever (INR 1.5 bn in FY26, INR 2+ bn in FY27). However, by Q3 FY26, management admitted they are only tracking INR 150 cr for the year and have de-prioritized motors because the generator business is consuming all capacity and attention. The motor business will grow at only 10-15% per year for now. This means a key diversification avenue is on the back burner.

### Red Flag 6: Promoter Holding Decline - Rating: 3/10

This was flagged by an analyst during Q1 FY26 concall. While the question got disconnected, promoter holding decline is worth monitoring as it could signal reduced skin in the game.

### Red Flag 7: Single-Dimensional Domestic Market - Rating: 4/10

India's domestic market is growing at only 4-10% annually and is limited to captive power plants (steam turbines for steel, cement) and small hydro. The AI data center opportunity in India is still "talking stage" per management, with no actual ground-level investment happening. Gas availability for data centers in India remains a question mark. This means domestic business remains a slow-growth segment for the foreseeable future.

## 5. Catalysts & DNA Shifts - What Can Drive the Stock Price Up

### Catalyst 1: Data Center / AI Power Boom (Impact: Very High)

This is the single biggest structural shift for TDPS. Global data centers and AI server farms are driving massive demand for gas turbine and gas engine generators. The trend is for data centers to move away from grid dependence towards captive power (own power plants). Power requirements per server farm are moving to 50-100+ MW, which can only be met by gas turbines/engines (not diesel). Management says this demand has visibility until 2030 with firm forecasts from OEMs. Grid stabilization demand in Europe (accelerated by the Spain/Portugal blackout) is competing for the same equipment. Germany alone has called for 20 GW of gas power for grid stabilization. This is a multi-year structural tailwind that is getting stronger, not weaker.

### Catalyst 2: Third Plant Coming Live - Capacity Step-Up (Impact: High)

The third plant was declared operational on December 18, 2025. This enables TDPS to ramp from INR 450 cr/quarter to INR 550-575 cr/quarter in Q4 FY26 and INR 600 cr/quarter in Q1 FY27 onwards. This is a classic "capex coming live" story. The additional capacity was needed desperately as the company was running at beyond-planned utilization with just 2 plants. The incremental revenue from this plant will flow with minimal additional fixed cost increase (most costs already absorbed in Q3 FY26), meaning significant operating leverage from Q4 FY26 onwards.

### Catalyst 3: Large Generator Entry (50-150 MW) via UK Design Center (Impact: High, Long-term)

This is a genuine DNA shift. TDPS has historically made generators up to 40-45 MW. With the UK design center, they are developing their own 50-150 MW generators. Currently, this large generator market is dominated by a single player. TDPS sees itself as the natural second entrant. Management says the addressable market for larger generators is equal to the size of their current market. The first prototype unit for a large generator was being delivered to customer for testing by end of December 2025/early January 2026. If qualification succeeds, volume orders could start in H2 FY27. Management described this as a "multi-hundred crore opportunity" that could be another big business for TDPS. This essentially doubles their total addressable market from FY28 onwards.

### Catalyst 4: Railway Export Contracts Going Live (Impact: Medium)

TDPS has secured railway traction motor contracts for 4 markets: India (Alstom, winding down by FY28), US, Europe, and Russia. Prototype units for US and Europe were delivered in Q3 FY26 for testing. Qualification is expected in Q4 FY26, with volume production starting Q1 FY27. The Russian market testing should complete by Q4 FY27. While the railway segment isn't growing explosively, the transition from a single domestic contract to multi-market exports de-risks the segment and provides a steady base.

### Catalyst 5: Hydro Refurbishment Business (Impact: Medium)

Hydro generator orders are at their highest in company history. The refurbishment market (both domestic and international) is a new vertical where TDPS can service generators regardless of original manufacturer. This is a higher-margin, recurring business opportunity. International hydro orders are coming from Nepal, Vietnam, and other markets. Domestic hydro refurbishment government tenders are also picking up. Good 2-3 year visibility in this segment.

### Catalyst 6: FX Tailwind from INR Depreciation (Impact: Medium-High)

With 79% of order inflows being export/deemed export, TDPS is a massive FX beneficiary. The company took a conscious decision to stop hedging about 6 months ago (only 10% of business hedged). With the dollar at INR 91-92 and Euro having strengthened significantly, the FX gains will flow to the bottom line from Q4 FY26 and particularly in Q1 FY27 onwards. This is a direct margin and PAT booster that the market may not be fully pricing in.

### Catalyst 7: CO2 Battery Storage / Energy Dome (Impact: Low-Medium, Early Stage)

TDPS is supplying generators for an Italian company's CO2 battery storage technology (Energy Dome). One of TDPS's major Indian customers has also invested in this technology. If CO2-based energy storage scales globally, it could become a meaningful new application for TDPS generators. Still early stage (pilot plants) but an interesting optionality.

### Catalyst 8: Nuclear Privatization in India (Impact: Low, Long-term)

If India opens nuclear power to the private sector, TDPS sees significant opportunity in the motor business for nuclear plants. However, the generator sizes (300-400 MW) are beyond TDPS's current capability. This is a motor-side optionality rather than a generator opportunity.

## 6. Valuation Context & Summary

### Key Strengths
The company is riding a structural multi-year upcycle driven by AI/data center power demand and grid stabilization needs globally. It has a clear cost advantage over European/Japanese peers, a strong balance sheet with zero debt and INR 1.93 bn cash, consistently upgraded guidance (every quarter), a new plant coming live adding 30-40% capacity, deep OEM relationships with 4-year demand visibility, and an optionality pipeline in large generators, rail exports, hydro refurb, and new energy technologies.

### Key Concerns
The business has high dependence on a few global OEMs, tariff uncertainty requires constant mitigation planning, weak free cash flow during this growth phase, the motor business has been sidelined, and there is limited domestic market growth potential in the near term.

### Overall Assessment
TDPS is in its strongest position since inception. The confluence of AI-driven power demand, grid stabilization needs, capacity expansion, and new product development creates a rare multi-lever growth story. The management under Nikhil Kumar is operationally focused, conservative in guidance (consistently beating and upgrading), and transparent about risks. The company's FY26 to FY28 trajectory of INR 1,800 cr to INR 2,800 cr represents a potential near-doubling of revenue with expanding margins, making it one of the most compelling capital goods growth stories in the Indian market.
