# Waaree Energies Limited - Deep Dive Analysis

*Based on Q1 FY26 (Jul 2025), Q2 FY26 (Oct 2025), Q3 FY26 (Jan 2026), and US Duty Discussion (Feb 2026) Conference Call Transcripts*

*Analysis Date: March 20, 2026*

## 1. Business Model

Waaree Energies is India's largest solar module manufacturer, transitioning from a pure-play solar module company into an integrated energy transition platform. The company operates across a growing value chain:

**Core Business - Solar Modules:** This remains the bread and butter. Waaree manufactures solar PV modules (both TOPCon and Mono PERC, rapidly shifting to TOPCon) across facilities in India and the US. The module business contributes roughly 80% of the order book value. The company has built a retail distribution network of 580+ stores and 4,000+ distribution points over a decade, which is an incredibly difficult moat to replicate. Retail contributes 20-25% of revenue at premium margins (1 to 1.5 cents per watt premium over institutional).

**Backward Integration - Cells, Ingots & Wafers:** The 5.4 GW cell facility at Chikhli (India's largest) became operational in Q1 FY26 and has ramped to ~80% utilization by Q3. A second 10 GW cell facility and 10 GW ingot-wafer facility are under construction for FY27 commissioning. This is the single most important strategic move by the company. Cell integration adds 300-400 bps to EBITDA margins on top of the module-only margin of 18-19%.

**US Manufacturing:** Currently 2.6 GW operational (including Meyer Burger acquisition), expanding to 4.2 GW by mid-CY2026. The US facility earns 45X IRA tax credits (c7 per watt peak) and commands higher realizations (26-30 cents vs 16-17 cents for non-DCR India modules). US revenue is already crossing Rs 2,000 Cr per quarter.

**Adjacencies (Waaree 2.0):**
- Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS): 20 GWh facility planned by FY28, investment of ~Rs 8,000 Cr. Revenue potential of $1.5-2 billion once operational.
- Inverters: 3 GW operational at Sarodhi, expanding to 4 GW. Phase 2 (1 GW) by FY27.
- Green Hydrogen Electrolysers: 1 GW facility, capex of Rs 676 Cr, FY27 commissioning. PLI of Rs 444 Cr secured.
- Transformers: Expanding to 20,000 MVA capacity, Rs 192 Cr capex. Already has Rs 245 Cr order book.
- Smart Meters: 76% stake in Racemosa Energy India.
- Renewable Power Infrastructure: PPAs worth 713 MW signed, 6.1 GW connectivity secured, 3,500+ acres of land acquired.

**EPC Business:** Through subsidiary Waaree Renewable Technologies, the EPC pipeline is 25+ GW. This is a high-margin, high-entry-barrier business. The wallet share strategy is brilliant: when Waaree was just selling modules, wallet share was 40-45%. With modules + EPC + batteries + inverters + connectivity + land, it jumps to 85-90%.

**Revenue Mix:** Approximately 65-70% domestic, 30-35% overseas. Within domestic, about 53% comes from the Indian market and the rest from retail and EPC. The order book split is roughly 60% overseas and 40% India by value, but sales can vary quarter to quarter.

## 4. What is Driving the Stock - The DNA Shift (Waaree 1.0 to Waaree 2.0)

This is the most critical section. Waaree is undergoing a fundamental transformation that justifies a re-rating:

**From Module Manufacturer to Integrated Energy Platform:** The company is building full backward integration from polysilicon (via Oman investment in United Solar Holdings) through ingots, wafers, cells, to modules. Simultaneously, it is expanding forward into BESS, inverters, transformers, smart meters, EPC, land, and connectivity. The vision stated by CEO Amit Paithankar is to become "the energy transition major of choice."

**ALMM for Cells (June 2026):** This is a massive catalyst. From June 1, 2026, all government solar projects (PM Surya Ghar, PM KUSUM, NTPC contracts, etc.) will require domestically manufactured cells. Waaree is the only company with 5.4 GW of operational cell capacity right now. The addressable DCR market for PM Surya Ghar alone has gone from 3 GW last year to 10 GW this year and is expected to be much more next year. Waaree targets 25-30% market share of this segment. DCR modules command Rs 23-24 per watt-peak vs Rs 14-15 for non-DCR, representing a significant premium.

**ALMM for Ingots & Wafers (June 2028):** The next regulatory tailwind. Companies without ingot-wafer capacity will potentially be locked out of 95-100% of the Indian market opportunity. Waaree's 10 GW ingot-wafer facility (FY27 commissioning) positions it as one of the top 2-3 players nationally.

**US Market Expansion - FEOC Compliance:** The FEOC (Foreign Entity of Concern) regulation is a game-changer for Waaree. From April 2026, the US is enforcing that no Chinese-owned entity (>30% Chinese holding even at the polysilicon level) can be part of the supply chain. Waaree has built a completely FEOC-compliant, non-Chinese, fully traceable supply chain. With US module demand projected at 70-80 GW annually (pipeline of 180-200 GW over next 2-3 years), and domestic US module capacity at only ~50 GW, India (and specifically Waaree) becomes the default supplier. Waaree already has one of the largest US order books among Indian manufacturers.

**45X IRA Tax Credits:** These manufacturing credits of c7 per watt peak are now confirmed to continue (retained even under the Big Beautiful Bill). The US operation is already generating meaningful IRA income (Rs 80-162 Cr per quarter), and this will scale as US capacity doubles.

**China Export Rebate Removal:** China's removal of export rebates on solar products is pushing cell prices up from c4-4.5 to c6 per watt-peak globally. This makes Indian manufacturing more cost-competitive and is structurally positive for Waaree.

**BESS - The Next Solar Story:** Management has drawn the parallel explicitly: just as solar went from expensive to cheapest energy source over a decade, battery storage is on a similar curve. With a Rs 8,000 Cr investment in a 20 GWh facility, Waaree is positioning for a potential $1.5-2 billion revenue stream. If ALMM-like policies come to BESS (which management expects), early movers will have a decisive advantage.

Only 30% comes from price-sensitive institutional domestic markets. This is a very defensible revenue mix.

## 6. Margin Architecture

Management has been very clear about how they manage margins:

**Module-only EBITDA margin (long-term sustainable):** 18-19%

**Cell integration kicker:** Additional 300-400 bps, taking blended module+cell margin to 22-25%

**DCR vs Non-DCR spread:** DCR commands 300-350 bps higher margin than non-DCR

**Gross margin management philosophy:** The company manages gross margin as its North Star. Input costs (cells, wafers, silver) are back-to-back tied against orders. When booking an order, cell procurement is simultaneously locked in. Silver impact is less than 9% on a module basis. Commodity risk is managed, not speculated on.

**US realizations:** 26-30 cents per watt (India non-DCR: 14-15 cents; India DCR: 23-24 cents per watt-peak). US margins are comparable to or slightly higher than India.

**FY27 Margin outlook:** As cell ramp-up completes, DCR share increases, retail grows, and US operations scale, the structural margin improvement should continue. Management expects 25% EBITDA margin to be sustained.

## 7. Red Flags

### Red Flag 1: US Anti-Dumping / Countervailing Duty Investigation - Rating: 8/10

This is the single biggest risk. The US Department of Commerce announced a preliminary CVD of 126% on Indian solar imports in February 2026. While management insists this does not impact them because their cells are sourced from ~10% tariff jurisdictions (not India), the investigation is ongoing and could take 6-12 months. The company has already made a provision of Rs 294 Cr in Q3 as a precautionary measure based on legal advice. If the final determination is unfavorable or scope expands, it could materially impact the US business which contributes 30-35% of revenue.

**Mitigant:** Waaree is rapidly expanding US manufacturing to 4.2 GW. For US-manufactured modules, cell sourcing jurisdiction tariff applies, and they have diversified to low-tariff regions. The 126% duty applies only to India-based cells used in modules exported to US, which Waaree does not use. Contracts have change-of-law clauses allowing pass-through.

### Red Flag 2: Massive Capex Execution Risk - Rating: 7/10

Rs 25,000+ Cr capex across multiple verticals (cells, ingots, wafers, BESS, inverters, electrolysers, transformers) simultaneously is an enormous execution challenge. This is being done across Gujarat, Maharashtra, and the US. Any delays in commissioning could affect revenue projections and return on capital.

**Mitigant:** Zero debt + Rs 7,500 Cr cash on balance sheet. Land is already secured for most projects. The 6 GW module expansion uses a pre-existing facility (just retrofitting), so timeline is shorter. Cell factory construction benefits from experience of building the first 5.4 GW. The company has won PLI incentives for ingots-wafers, cells, and modules, adding a financial cushion.

### Red Flag 3: Technology Obsolescence Risk - Rating: 5/10

TOPCon is the current dominant technology, but XBC and HJT are gaining share. If a rapid technology shift occurs (like perovskite reaching commercial viability), the massive capex on current-gen facilities could face accelerated depreciation. Technology in solar changes every 3-4 years.

**Mitigant:** Waaree's Meyer Burger acquisition gives them HJT capability in the US. Their production lines are designed to be flexible across technologies. The company is actively investing in R&D with IIT Bombay on perovskite technology. The BESS facility is designed to handle both lithium-ion and sodium-ion chemistry.

### Red Flag 4: Silver Price / Commodity Risk - Rating: 4/10

Silver constitutes about 25% of cell manufacturing cost. Silver prices have been rising, and some analysts worry about gross margin compression. Chinese export rebate removal is pushing cell prices higher globally.

**Mitigant:** Silver impact is less than 9% on a module basis. The company does back-to-back procurement against orders. Operating leverage and efficiency gains from scale can offset commodity headwinds. Management has consistently maintained 25% EBITDA margin through multiple commodity cycles.

### Red Flag 5: Promoter Holding Transfer - Rating: 4/10

There was a promoter holding transfer within the family entity that came as a surprise to investors. While management said it was a family structuring matter, any lack of transparency on promoter-level decisions is a yellow flag for corporate governance watchers.

**Mitigant:** The company is listed, audited, and transparent in its disclosures. The transfer did not involve dilution to public shareholders.

### Red Flag 6: Cell Ramp-Up Has Been Slow - Rating: 4/10

The 5.4 GW cell facility was inaugurated about a year ago but only reached 80% utilization (on a running-rate basis) by Q3 FY26. On a full-quarter average basis, utilization was still 55-60%. Teething issues (effluent, breakages, equipment fine-tuning) caused delays.

**Mitigant:** Management says most teething troubles are behind them. They are upgrading to G12R cells which should push utilization above 85-90% within 3-4 months. The learning from 5.4 GW will accelerate the next 10 GW facility build.

### Red Flag 7: BESS/Adjacencies Are Unproven - Rating: 5/10

The Rs 8,000 Cr BESS investment is in an embryonic stage. No ALMM-like policy exists for batteries yet. Management cannot give hard revenue or margin numbers. Battery technology changes rapidly. The payback period and ROCE are uncertain. If the BESS investment does not deliver expected returns, it represents a significant capital misallocation risk.

**Mitigant:** Battery storage demand in India is projected to grow to 236 GWh by 2032. PLI and viability gap funding discussions are underway. The company's payback framework targets 3-5 years for manufacturing capex. They raised Rs 1,000 Cr specifically for this through equity.

### Red Flag 8: Geographic Concentration Risk (US + India) - Rating: 3/10

Nearly 95% of revenue comes from just two markets. Any policy change in either (India rolling back ALMM, US imposing broader tariffs) could be disruptive.

**Mitigant:** The company is actively exploring EU (Germany, Italy), Middle East, Africa, Australia, GCC, and Pacific markets. EPC business in Middle East and Africa is growing. They follow Indian government trade agreement cues for market entry.

## 10. Conclusion

Waaree Energies is in the middle of a structural transformation from a solar module manufacturer to a fully integrated energy transition platform. The financial trajectory is exceptional: revenue tripling in 3 quarters, EBITDA margins expanding 700+ bps year-on-year, zero debt, and Rs 7,500 Cr cash. The regulatory environment in both India (ALMM) and the US (FEOC, 45X) is strongly favorable for an Indian manufacturer with full backward integration and a diversified non-Chinese supply chain.

The biggest near-term risks are the US duty investigation and execution on the massive Rs 25,000 Cr capex plan. However, the company's zero-debt balance sheet, strong cash generation, and management's track record of consistently beating guidance provide comfort.

On a multi-year view, Waaree is positioning itself to capture what is genuinely a multi-decadal opportunity in energy transition. The combination of India's domestic solar capacity target (280 GW by 2030), US data center and AI-driven power demand (70-80 GW annual module demand), and the company's unique positioning as one of the few FEOC-compliant, fully traceable, integrated manufacturers globally creates a compelling growth runway.
